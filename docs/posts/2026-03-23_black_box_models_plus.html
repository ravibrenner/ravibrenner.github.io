Looking into the Black Box

general
Author

Ravi Brenner

Published

March 23, 2026

I have been reading a bit more about deep learning recently (future post to come), and was trying to connect it to the bigger picture of how we use computers to learn from data.

Generally speaking, deep learning is a special type of machine learning, which is itself a special type of AI (artificial intelligence). Confusingly, when most people talk about AI, they are referring to generative AI and large language models, which are really special types of deep learning models (so they would be inside the innermost circle below):

Often, people refer to these deep learning models as “Black Box” models.

A black box model is one where we can see the inputs and the outputs, but where the intermediate steps are too hard to see or understand. The data goes into the black box and comes out the other side, and we can’t understand anything in between.

I say ‘understand’ because we actually can see it most of the time. Even for deep learning models and large language models, we can technically extract the values of all the neurons at any given step. But they are considered uninterpretable, at least to human eyes. The models are so large, and the internal math so long, that we can’t understand what is happening.

The opposite of this would be a ‘white’ or ‘glass’ box model, one where we can interpret what is going on more easily. Some of the more conventional machine learning models (like logistic regression or decision trees) are more like this. We can see and understand what is happening at each step.

LLMs take the black box concept a step further, because they actually enforce randomness in the model outputs. When the model is predicting the next word it should write, it doesn’t always use the most probable output. It randomly selects from the possible next words instead.

This leads to a situation where each input and output pair from a large language model is essentially a tiny experiment, with sample size of 1.

War with words

This is why it is so shocking to me that the U.S. military is reportedly using Anthropic’s Claude for the ongoing war with Iran.

Large language models are impressive. Certainly I would expect that software engineers and data scientists in the military are using generative AI to speed up writing new code. But this is apparently not what is going on:

“The Pentagon uses AI in the ways many consumers do — to summarize and distill lots of information at once. According to former Pentagon officials, by analyzing documents, video, and images coming in from the battlefield, AI can help the military war-game out scenarios to minimize casualties and determine which weapons can be most effective.”

I am not a military planning expert, but this is not how I would expect or want the military to be using AI. What if they run the same data through the model twice, or 10 times, or 100 times? Will it give the same responses about where to strike, and when? What if the prompts are changed, are the responses the same? If you ask Claude to pick military targets based on some inputs, it will do it. But is that the right question to be asking it?

This brings to mind the classic quote by statistician George Box:

“All models are wrong but some are useful”

LLMs can be useful for some tasks, but they are fundamentally the wrong tool for high-stakes decision making. Only some models are useful for a given task, and choosing the right one is important. Unfortunately when you have a hammer, everything looks like a nail.

We now have access to such a wide range of tools for learning from data. Large language models (and vision/audio models) are undoubtedly a powerful part of the toolkit now. But they have serious tradeoffs in terms of specialization, controllability, randomness, predictability, and honesty that can’t be resolved. Not being able to look into the black box is a serious drawback. There is still an important place for conventional machine learning models, deep learning models trained for specific purposes, simulation models, system dynamic models, and statistics.

Asking the right question, and knowing how to get the correct answer, is more important than ever.

Borges

Two quotes from the author Jorge Luis Borges that got me thinking about this more:

“A man sets out to draw the world. As the years go by, he peoples a space with images of provinces, kingdoms, mountains, bays, ships, islands, fishes, rooms, instruments, stars, horses, and individuals. A short time before he dies, he discovers that the patient labyrinth of lines traces the lineaments of his own face.” (The Aleph and Other Stories).

And the short story, On Exactitude in science:

“…In that Empire, the Art of Cartography attained such Perfection that the map of a single Province occupied the entirety of a City, and the map of the Empire, the entirety of a Province. In time, those Unconscionable Maps no longer satisfied, and the Cartographers Guilds struck a Map of the Empire whose size was that of the Empire, and which coincided point for point with it. The following Generations, who were not so fond of the Study of Cartography as their Forebears had been, saw that that vast Map was Useless, and not without some Pitilessness was it, that they delivered it up to the Inclemencies of Sun and Winters. In the Deserts of the West, still today, there are Tattered Ruins of that Map, inhabited by Animals and Beggars; in all the Land there is no other Relic of the Disciplines of Geography.”
